Abstract

Vision-based imitation learning has achieved remarkable success in robotic manipulation. However, contact-rich tasks demand real-time force feedback, as vision alone cannot capture intricate contact dynamics. Existing methods pursue two directions: feature-level fusion concatenates visual and force representations within shared backbones, yet suffers from modality collapse where high-dimensional visual features dominate optimization and suppress force signals; policy-level fusion merges separate policy outputs in action space, yet is often phase-agnostic—relying on fixed or discrete combination that cannot adapt to contact phase.

We present FCAR, an architecture that addresses both limitations through action-space decoupling and a learnable adaptive gate. FCAR uses a vision-proprioception base policy for nominal motion and a force-conditioned residual for contact correction, fused by a gate that allocates authority from interaction state without contact labels. By isolating modalities until the action space, FCAR structurally prevents modality collapse while enabling seamless phase transitions. Experiments on real-world peg-in-hole insertion, drawer manipulation, and surface wiping show 88.3% average success rate, outperforming the strongest feature-level and policy-level baselines by 11.6 percentage points, with strong generalization to unseen physical conditions.

Results

We evaluate FCAR on a 7-DoF Franka Panda with joint torque sensing and a third-person RGB camera. Three contact-rich tasks: Peg-in-Hole (PIH), Drawer, and Wiping. FCAR reaches 88.3% average success rate (90% PIH, 90% Drawer, 85% Wiping), outperforming FoAR by 11.6 percentage points and the heuristic-gated policy-level baseline by a large margin. Ablations confirm the necessity of the residual branch, modality decoupling, learned gate, and two-stage training. FCAR also generalizes to unseen positions, initial offsets, and surface conditions (81.3% on OOD settings).
